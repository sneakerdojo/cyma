## Why frameworks belong in this document

We don't use frameworks because they're impressive. We use them as **decision compressors** — when faced with a choice (e.g., should we open with paid ads or with content?), the framework gives us a fast, reasoned default. This appendix summarises the four frameworks referenced throughout for quick lookup.

---

## 1. Hormozi $100M Offers — The Value Equation

**One-line summary:** make an offer so good that people feel stupid saying no.

### The equation

$$
\text{Value} = \frac{\text{Dream Outcome} \times \text{Perceived Likelihood of Achievement}}{\text{Time Delay} \times \text{Effort \& Sacrifice}}
$$

| Lever | Question to ask |
|---|---|
| Dream outcome | What is the customer ACTUALLY trying to achieve? |
| Perceived likelihood | How can we make the outcome more believable? |
| Time delay | How can we deliver the outcome faster? |
| Effort & sacrifice | How can we make the customer do less work? |

### The Grand Slam Offer structure

1. **Identify the dream outcome** — what they want, in their words
2. **List every problem** that prevents them from achieving it
3. **Convert each problem into a solution** delivered by your offer
4. **Stack the solutions** as bonuses with explicit dollar values
5. **Add risk reversal** (guarantee, refund, free pilot)
6. **Set scarcity / urgency** (limited spots, deadline)
7. **Name it** (so it's referrable, not just generic)

### How Octio applies it

The Lead Gen R8,500/mo offer is constructed as a Grand Slam:

| Element | Octio implementation |
|---|---|
| Dream outcome | "Every lead captured 24/7 with no new hires" |
| Stack | Lead Gen + audit + brand voice tuning + prompt updates + CRM integration |
| Bonuses | Patient Zero proof, founder onboarding, weekly digest |
| Risk reversal | 14-day money-back |
| Urgency | First-cohort pricing (3-month lock-in) |
| Name | "AI Lead Generation Starter" |

## 2. Hormozi $100M Leads — The Core Four

**One-line summary:** there are only four ways to generate leads at scale; master them in order.

### The four channels

| Channel | Description | Time to ROI |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm outreach** | DMs/calls to people who already know you | Days |
| **Content** | Posting publicly on platforms (LinkedIn, YouTube, etc.) | Months |
| **Cold outreach** | Email/DM/SMS to strangers | Weeks |
| **Paid ads** | Buying attention on Meta, Google, LinkedIn | Days (but costly) |

### The sequencing principle

You don't run all four simultaneously from day 1. Sequence:

1. **Warm first** — your existing network is undermonetised; talk to them
2. **Content second** — start building distribution; takes months to compound
3. **Cold third** — once messaging is tested, scale outbound
4. **Paid fourth** — only after CAC < LTV/3 is proven from warmer channels

### Lead magnets

Hormozi argues the lead magnet should give a complete, valuable thing that demonstrates "you can solve a real problem for free; imagine what we do for paying customers."

Octio's lead magnet: the Audit Tool. Not a PDF, not a webinar, not a checklist. An actual AI consulting engagement, free.

### How Octio applies it

| Phase | Channels active | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Month 0–3 | Warm + audit funnel | No brand recognition; warm is highest-leverage |
| Month 3–6 | + Content + small paid | Brand begins to compound; test paid CAC |
| Month 6+ | + Cold + scaled paid + partners | Full stack active, ordered by efficiency |

## 3. Crossing the Chasm — The Bowling Alley

**One-line summary:** the gap between early adopters and mainstream is real; the path across is to pick one niche, dominate it, then expand to adjacent niches.

### The technology adoption curve

```
  Innovators     Early adopters   ← the chasm →   Early majority   Late majority   Laggards
  (2.5%)         (13.5%)                          (34%)            (34%)            (16%)
```

The chasm is where many startups die — early adopters loved you for being novel, but mainstream customers don't care about novelty; they care about pragmatic value.

### The bowling alley strategy

After crossing the chasm with your beachhead niche, you don't try to attack the whole mainstream market. You knock down adjacent "pins" — niches that are similar to your beachhead but in adjacent industries or use cases.

### How Octio applies it

| Pin | Niche | Why it follows |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (beachhead) | Service businesses with after-hours pain (plumbers, electricians) | Highest pain, most legible ROI |
| 2 | Medical practices (dentists, vets, GPs) | Same underlying pain; tighter compliance |
| 3 | Legal + accounting firms | Higher willingness to pay; longer sales cycle |
| 4 | Coaches, consultants, agencies | Adjacent service category; different product mix |

We don't try to sell to all four pins at once. We dominate pin 1, then pin 2, then pin 3 in sequence.

## 4. Lean Canvas (Ash Maurya)

**One-line summary:** a one-page business plan tuned for startups — 9 cells that force you to articulate problem, solution, customer, channels, revenue, and unfair advantage.

### The 9 cells

| Cell | Question |
|---|---|
| 1. Problem | What top 3 problems are you solving? |
| 2. Customer Segments | Who are your customers? |
| 3. Unique Value Proposition | Why are you different and worth paying attention to? |
| 4. Solution | What's your top 3 features? |
| 5. Channels | How do you reach customers? |
| 6. Revenue Streams | How do you make money? |
| 7. Cost Structure | What are your fixed and variable costs? |
| 8. Key Metrics | What do you measure? |
| 9. Unfair Advantage | What can't be easily copied or bought? |

### Octio's Lean Canvas (filled)

| Cell | Octio |
|---|---|
| Problem | SMBs lose leads to slow response; can't afford to hire; can't operationalise AI tools themselves |
| Segments | SA service businesses (plumbers, dentists, etc.); 250k SAM |
| UVP | "The AI company that built itself — we run our own AI products on our own ops" |
| Solution | 4 autonomous AI products + 3 services + free audit tool |
| Channels | Audit funnel + LinkedIn + newsletter + warm + paid (sequenced) |
| Revenue | Subscription R3.5k–R18.5k/mo + project fees R85k+ |
| Costs | API + infra + Twilio + Stripe ~12% of revenue; OPEX ~R13k/mo solo |
| Metrics | MRR, customer count, churn, CAC, LTV/CAC, audit-to-customer conversion |
| Unfair advantage | Patient Zero proof + portfolio depth + SA-local + recursive sales |

### Cite

- Ash Maurya, *Running Lean* (2010 / 2nd ed. 2012)

## How these frameworks layer together

Each framework solves a different layer:

| Question | Framework |
|---|---|
| What do we sell, and how do we make the offer irresistible? | $100M Offers |
| Where do leads come from? | $100M Leads (Core Four) |
| Which segment do we target first, and how do we sequence growth? | Crossing the Chasm (Bowling Alley) |
| How does it all fit on one page? | Lean Canvas |

Used together, they cover the GTM stack without redundancy. We don't use Porter's Five Forces (too academic for our stage), nor Jobs-To-Be-Done (overlapping with $100M Offers), nor Business Model Canvas (Lean Canvas is the leaner sibling for startups).
